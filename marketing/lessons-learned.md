# Lessons learned: our playbook

Distillation of [case-studies/](case-studies/README.md) into the decisions we
are actually making. The strategic choice: **we follow the Secret Benefits
model** — quiet growth via affiliates, SEO harvesting, and product-led
marketplace mechanics, with no founder-as-lightning-rod and no engineered
controversy. This document records what that means concretely, what we borrow
from the loud playbook anyway, what we explicitly reject, and where we must
deviate from Secret Benefits because we are a UK-registered, UK-regulated
company.

[channels.md](channels.md) and [launch-plan.md](launch-plan.md) have been
updated to implement this playbook — "What this changed in our other docs"
at the end records the deltas.

## The model in one paragraph

Secret Benefits became a top-3 global player (~4-8M visits/month by
independent estimates) with no named founder, no press office, no app, and
zero paid search. Its acquisition machine: affiliates and review sites fight
and win the non-brand SEO war ("best sugar dating sites"), the brand harvests
the resulting navigational search (~368K monthly searches for its own name);
a credits-not-subscription model monetises peak intent without renewal
grievances; the supply side is free; video verification converts trust
anxiety into a conversion asset; and being web-only made both app-store
purges irrelevant while keeping 100% of payment revenue. Full detail and
evidence quality in [case-studies/secret-benefits.md](case-studies/secret-benefits.md).

## Core commitments

1. **Web/PWA-only, own billing, forever.** The app-era purge
   ([case-studies/app-era-purge.md](case-studies/app-era-purge.md)) showed
   store-dependent brands erased with 30 days' notice while every web-first
   incumbent survived. We never build a store dependence, including "just a
   companion app" — the PWA is the product. Payments run through our own
   high-risk-capable processor (see [README.md](README.md)), never store IAP.

2. **The affiliate/review ecosystem is our primary acquisition channel —
   for both sides of the marketplace.** Supply-side members google their
   way in through the same listicles ("best sugar dating sites", "how to
   find a sugar daddy") that deliver the demand side; what differs per side
   is the economics. Concretely:
   - Get listed in the "best sugar dating sites" listicles that already
     own the money keywords, prioritising UK-facing ones; the ecosystem is
     smaller than the US one Secret Benefits rides, so early placements are
     winnable. Equip affiliates for the supply-side audience too ("for
     sugar babies" sections, safety/verification talking points).
   - Run the affiliate programme direct/invite-only (as Secret Benefits
     appears to), not on public CPA network rate cards — we keep control of
     who promotes us and how.
   - Demand-side payouts: CPA on credit purchases (aligning affiliate
     incentives with real revenue). Supply-side payouts: realistically we
     must pay for verified female activations too — gender/geo-tiered CPL
     is industry standard — but never on raw signups: payable only on video
     verification plus an activity window, with delayed payout, clawbacks,
     and per-affiliate caps. The bounty is priced from the demand-side
     revenue an active verified profile generates.
   - Approved-creatives clause, enforced. Two studies supply the warning:
     rogue affiliate copy is an ASA liability that attaches to us, and
     affiliates kept publishing fake-fresh "reviews" of RichMeetBeautiful
     years after it died. We police the channel or it poisons us.
   - What affiliates cannot do is concentrate supply in a launch city —
     the supply-side density layer in channels.md (referral, trust
     content, student media) exists for that, not because listicles can't
     deliver women.

3. **Brand-harvest SEO, not content-empire SEO.** Secret Benefits does not
   rank for non-brand terms; its affiliates do. We invert our earlier
   SEO-heavy plan accordingly: a memorable, clean brand name; own every
   navigational SERP (brand, "brand login", "brand review", "is brand
   legit") with our own pages before affiliates or scrapers do; a modest
   blog for long-tail trust queries; city pages stay (cheap, UK-tractable)
   but are no longer the flagship investment.

4. **Product decisions are the marketing.** Adopted directly:
   - **Credits, not subscriptions**, for the paying side. Prices peak
     intent, uncaps whale spend, and avoids the auto-renewal/chargeback
     surface that draws FTC/CMA-class scrutiny onto subscription dating.
     Feed this into the token-economy plan — and bundle/cap costs for heavy
     users, because "money pit" reviews are the model's known failure mode.
   - **Free for the supply side.** Standard marketplace subsidy; every
     surviving competitor does it.
   - **Visible verification.** Video-selfie verification badge, prominently
     surfaced in search results and profiles. It is conversion marketing,
     scam-anxiety relief, and Online Safety Act groundwork in one feature.
     The already-planned secret album fits the same discretion-as-product
     positioning.
   - **Discretion framing.** "Secret"/private mechanics are the brand
     promise that lets users self-describe membership comfortably — which is
     also why referral in this category must be private link/code sharing,
     not social broadcast.

5. **Positioning discipline everywhere metadata lives.** Google banned apps
   by stated purpose, not user behaviour. Our name, copy, affiliate
   creatives, and any future store or ad-network listing stay a dating
   proposition. No "compensated", no "arrangement", no money-for-intimacy
   language, anywhere. This is what keeps the surviving channels (X,
   ad networks, review sites) open and is cheaper than a forced rebrand
   later (Seeking's retcon; Sudy's unevidenced "elite" relaunch).

## Borrowed from the loud playbook (the modest floor)

The quiet model's one structural weakness is channel concentration: a Google
crackdown on sponsored listicles, an affiliate-network shift, or one adverse
investigation could destabilise the whole machine. Secret Benefits accepts
that risk; we hedge it cheaply:

- **The email list and the PWA install base are the owned floor.** Every
  channel lands in owned retention (see [channels.md](channels.md) wiring
  section). This is non-negotiable regardless of model.
- **Honest data PR, later, as an option — not a launch dependency.** Once
  the user base supports defensible aggregate stats, an annual UK report is
  the category's proven highest-ROI earned channel (Seeking, Ashley
  Madison). We keep a named, accountable spokesperson available for
  reactive press (journalists will find a UK company via Companies House
  anyway — better to answer well than to stonewall badly). What we take from
  the loud studies is the mechanic (recurring, localised, data-driven), not
  the provocation.
- **Publish only numbers a fact-checker would pass.** Every competitor's
  inflated member counts eventually became a liability. Being the one
  operator whose numbers verify is a differentiator we get for free by not
  lying.

## Explicitly rejected

- **Outrage/stunt marketing and engineered rejection.** RichMeetBeautiful
  converted a cheap billboard format into a criminal conviction, personal
  director liability, and a national ISP block; the Reflex stunt brands
  mostly died once the outrage cycle moved on. Post-2019 ASA gender-
  stereotype rules make even the soft version banned territory in the UK.
- **Desperation/debt-relief copy aimed at students.** The precise line is
  mapped in [case-studies/richmeetbeautiful.md](case-studies/richmeetbeautiful.md)
  and our guardrails in [README.md](README.md) stand: structural incentives
  and agency framing only; student debt is never a purchase trigger.
- **Fake profiles or seeded engagement, in any form.** Ashley Madison's
  fembots were consumer fraud with an FTC settlement attached, and the
  permanent second line of every article about the brand. Density comes from
  the seeding plan in [launch-plan.md](launch-plan.md), pricing, and
  concierge onboarding — never fabrication. Secret Benefits' persistent
  fake-profile complaints (3.4/5 Trustpilot) are the model's reputation
  ceiling; verification-by-default is how we beat the model we're copying.
- **Corporate anonymity.** Unavailable to us (Companies House) and not
  actually desirable: it forfeits trust, partnerships, and regulatory
  goodwill. Our version of "quiet" is low-profile and accountable, not
  hidden.
- **Escort-adjacent traffic buys.** Secret Benefits' largest referrer is an
  adult escort directory. For a UK operator under the Online Safety Act and
  ASA, placements that blur the dating/sex-work line are a positioning and
  legal risk that outweighs the traffic. Adult ad networks remain testable
  (per [channels.md](channels.md)) but placement quality is vetted, not
  bought blind.

## Where we deviate from Secret Benefits by necessity

| Secret Benefits | Us | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Offshore, anonymous operator | UK company, named directors | Companies House; and trust is our differentiator |
| No earned-media surface at all | Reactive press capability + optional annual data report | UK press will find us; concentration hedge |
| US-led traffic, global sprawl | UK-first, city-by-city density | Marketplace liquidity beats breadth ([launch-plan.md](launch-plan.md)) |
| Tolerates fake-profile complaints | Verification-by-default, moderation budget | OSA compliance; beats the model's known weakness |
| Silent on safety | Safety-forward content and onboarding | Legal necessity that doubles as supply-side acquisition copy |

## What this changed in our other docs (applied)

- **[channels.md](channels.md) restructured:** affiliates/review sites are
  the primary channel (#1), serving both sides with per-side economics;
  demand-side channels (switcher SEO, ad networks, X) and a city-targetable
  supply-side density layer (referral, trust content, student media,
  podcasts) sit beneath it, with brand-harvest SEO, product-led trust, and
  reactive press as cross-side infrastructure. Digital PR dropped from
  "category superpower" to a reactive capability plus optional annual
  report. OOH is out entirely (a loud-playbook tool with the worst risk
  profile in our category). Ad-network tests survive with the
  placement-vetting caveat.
- **[launch-plan.md](launch-plan.md) Phase 2** is now a quiet open launch:
  affiliate listings switching on, waitlist conversion, and founding-member
  unlocks are the levers; the press kit exists for reactive use only.
- **[README.md](README.md) payments note hardened:** the credits model is a
  strategic commitment, so the high-risk processor decision blocks the
  token-economy plan and should be resolved first (now reflected in
  launch-plan Phase 0).
- **Product backlog:** video verification and heavy-user cost caps join the
  discretion features (secret album) as marketing-critical, not
  nice-to-have.

## The metrics that decide everything

1. **Cost per activated, verified supply-side profile in a target city**,
   per channel, attributed in Postgres — also the exact event the affiliate
   supply-side CPL pays on, so metric and payout gate are one measurement.
2. **Cost per activated demand-side profile and per first credit purchase**,
   per channel.
3. **Branded search volume for our name** — the Secret Benefits model's
   single best proxy for whether the quiet machine is compounding. Track it
   monthly from launch; it is the KPI that tells us the affiliates' work is
   accruing to us and not just to them.
