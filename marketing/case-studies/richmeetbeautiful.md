# Case study: RichMeetBeautiful

## Overview

RichMeetBeautiful was a "sugar dating" platform (self-described "sugar daddy and sugar baby dating") founded by Norwegian entrepreneur Sigurd Vedal and operated through his Digisec Media group (Malta/Cyprus-registered entities; Digisec also ran the infidelity site Victoria Milan) [1][8]. Launched in Scandinavia in August 2017, it became briefly famous — and legally infamous — for a single acquisition tactic: mobile billboards driven past university campuses telling students to date a "sugar daddy" instead of taking student loans. The stunt was repeated near-identically in Oslo, Gothenburg, Brussels and Paris in a six-week window in autumn 2017, generating worldwide earned media, a criminal conviction in Belgium, an aggravated-pimping investigation in France, and a nationwide ISP block in Belgium [1][4][5]. The site is now dead: as of July 2026 its homepage redirects to a page stating "RichMeetBeautiful has been shut down" [16].

## Launch and early growth

- The site launched in Denmark, Norway, Sweden and Finland in August 2017, with YouTube and radio advertising followed by out-of-home billboards in Oslo [8][21]. Positioning: connect "beautiful" young women (and nominally men) with wealthy older "sugar daddies/mamas"; Vedal framed it as mentorship-plus-romance: "Our vision is to offer beautiful women a relationship to a mature, intelligent man, who can be a partner, conversation partner and mentor. In a relationship, it is also natural to have sex." [8]
- Expansion followed the billboard controversy city by city: Belgium (September 2017), France (October 2017), with announced plans for the UK (November 2017) and, after the Belgian ban, reported relocation of marketing effort to France and Switzerland [2][5][11].
- Growth claims are company-sourced and unverified: Vedal claimed roughly 60,000 members within six weeks of launch, about three-quarters women and roughly 68 per cent aged 18-26, as reported in Scandinavian press citing the company [10][19]. In Belgium the company reportedly estimated a potential of 300,000 members [12]. Vedal separately claimed 75 per cent of new sign-ups were women aged 18-26 [11]. Treat all of these as marketing claims, not audited figures.
- The explicit targeting of 18-26-year-old female students was not an accident of media placement; it was the stated recruitment demographic in the company's own statements and creative [12][11].

## Marketing playbook

The core play was a cheap, repeatable OOH shock format: a trailer-mounted billboard towed around university campuses at the start of the academic year, pairing student-debt anxiety with sugar dating.

What ran, where, with messaging as reported:

- Oslo, early September 2017: a "rolling billboard" showing a scantily clad woman with copy urging students to "Date a sugar daddy" and suggesting "0 kr" in student loans; roughly ten trailer billboards reportedly circulated around Oslo, Akershus and Baerum [9][19]. Oslo's mayor had police remove it — it breached both a city ordinance against rolling billboards and, per the Consumer Ombudsman's review, potentially the Marketing Act [9][8].
- Gothenburg, September 2017: campaign at Chalmers University of Technology with the slogan "Noll kronor i studielan? Dejta en sugar daddy" ("Zero kronor in student loans? Date a sugar daddy"); students complained to the Swedish Advertising Ombudsman and the student union prepared a police report [10].
- Brussels, late September 2017: trucks circulated around the Universite Libre de Bruxelles (ULB) and other higher-education institutions as students returned from summer break. Creative showed a woman's torso in a bra with slogans reported as "Hey female students, improve your lifestyle, go out with a Sugar Daddy" and (in French) "Un pret etudiant? Sortez avec un sugar daddy" ("A student loan? Date a sugar daddy") [1][4][12][20]. A truck was seized after ULB complained to police [1].
- Paris, 23-26 October 2017: a trailer billboard reading "Hey, les etudiant(e)s! Romantique, passion et pas de pret etudiant. Sortez avec un Sugar Daddy ou Sugar Mama" ("Hey students! Romance, passion and no student loan. Date a Sugar Daddy or Sugar Mama") toured Paris streets, stopping in front of Universite Paris-Descartes in the Latin Quarter; the trailer was seized in the 15th arrondissement for displaying advertising without prefectural authorisation, and a man assisting with the billboard was arrested [5][6][7].
- UK (announced, apparently never executed at scale): in November 2017 the company said it aimed to recruit 100,000 "sugar babies" from UK universities via a three-month campaign at more than 12 institutions including King's College London and LSE [11]. A student-press piece also referenced billboards at KCL, LSE and The City Law School [22]. No reporting of UK vans being seized, and no ASA ruling against RichMeetBeautiful could be found (see below) — the UK push appears to have been mostly a press-release-driven announcement riding the continental controversy.

Was the backlash intentional? Vedal never publicly admitted a deliberate outrage strategy; his public line was consistent denial and surprise — "There's nothing illegal about this"; the ads were "gender-neutral" because they addressed both women and men; "Prostitution is not promoted, but the financial aspect is part of any relationship"; he was reportedly "surprised" by the reaction and felt the world had "come further" [7][9][10]. But the pattern is telling: the identical stunt was re-run in a new capital within weeks of each ban (Oslo, September; Gothenburg, September; Brussels, late September; Paris, October), and each removal or seizure was itself a news event. AFP later characterised the campus posters as "a publicity coup" [1]. French reviewers noted the stunt "propelled the popularity" of the site [11].

Cost vs earned value: the format itself was very cheap (a handful of trailer billboards and drivers). It bought coverage from AFP, Newsweek, IBTimes, SBS, France24, Euronews, national broadcasters in Norway, Sweden, Belgium and France, and pickup as far away as Indonesia and Australia [1][6][7][13][20] — plus company-claimed sign-up surges [10]. The eventual price: seized vehicles in three countries, an advertising-ethics condemnation, a criminal prosecution, roughly 265,000 euros in fines and damages, a permanent site block in Belgium, and a founder with a criminal record for incitement to prostitution [4]. No credible reporting supports the idea that the trade-off paid off long-term; the site is gone.

Other channels: YouTube pre-roll and radio spots at the Scandinavian launch [8]; PR-driven earned media (the UK "100,000 sugar babies" announcement, ongoing press interviews) [11]; and the affiliate-review ecosystem typical of the dating vertical — dozens of "review" sites carried RichMeetBeautiful listings for years, some still publishing "reviews" of the site in 2025-2026, well after it shut down [16][23]. No verified reporting of TV buys or influencer programmes was found.

## Platform and regulatory run-ins

- Norway (September 2017): Oslo authorities ordered the rolling billboard removed as illegal under the city's ban on rolling billboards and national marketing law; the Consumer Ombudsman (Elisabeth Lier Haugseth) announced a review under the Marketing Act; the Christian Democrat MP Kjell Ingolf Ropstad called it "glorification of prostitution", and a prominent lawyer publicly suggested the ads could breach Norway's pimping provision ("hallikparagrafen") [8][9][19]. No Norwegian prosecution resulted, as far as reporting shows.
- Sweden (September 2017): complaints to the Advertising Ombudsman (Reklamombudsmannen) over the Chalmers campaign; a student-union police report was prepared. No known sanction beyond the reputational hit [10].
- Belgium (2017-2019) — the decisive jurisdiction. The Jury d'Ethique Publicitaire (advertising ethics jury) received a flood of complaints and ruled the campaign contrary to human dignity; Vedal announced withdrawal of the Belgian campaign on 28 September 2017 [12][20][21]. Brussels prosecutors opened a criminal case; Vedal was charged with incitement to debauchery and prostitution [1]. In March 2018 a Belgian court reportedly ordered ISPs to block the site [11]. The trial opened before the Brussels criminal court in October 2018 (postponed to February 2019) [11][4]. In April 2019 prosecutors sought a six-month suspended sentence plus a 40,000 euro fine for Vedal and 250,000 euros for Digisec; the prosecutor argued: "Even if weasel words were used, everyone knew what it was about. There are half-dressed women on the site, there are no photos of couples in a restaurant." [1][3] Verdict, 8 May 2019 (Brussels correctional court): guilty of incitement to debauchery/prostitution and distributing advertising inciting prostitution; six months' suspended prison for Vedal; a personal fine reported by RTBF as roughly 20,000-24,000 euros (several English-language outlets reported 200,000 euros — the RTBF figure is likely the accurate one); 240,000 euros fine for Digisec; 5,000 euros damages to ULB; permanent confiscation of the already-blocked site in Belgium. Notably, the court acquitted the defendants under Belgium's 2014 anti-sexism law, finding the ads did not target identifiable individuals [4][2][13].
- France (2017, unresolved): after the Paris seizure, the Paris prosecutor opened an investigation for aggravated pimping (proxenetisme aggrave), assigned to the anti-pimping brigade; Paris city hall filed a complaint seeking prosecution and closure of the site; deputy mayor Helene Bidard called the site "a form of violence against women" and Higher Education Minister Frederique Vidal called the ads "nothing more than invitation to prostitution" [5][6]. As of May 2019 the investigation was still reported as ongoing; no French conviction or formal closure decision was found in later reporting [13][14].
- United Kingdom: no ASA ruling against RichMeetBeautiful could be located in the ASA rulings ecosystem or press coverage; the announced UK campus campaign does not appear to have triggered regulatory action. (For context on where UK regulation now sits: the ASA banned a Seeking.com ad in October 2024 under the harmful-gender-stereotypes rules — the closest UK precedent for this category [25].)
- United States (2018-2022, commercial rather than regulatory): Reflex Media, operator of SeekingArrangement, sued RichMeetBeautiful Holding Ltd, Digisec Media and Vedal in the District of Nevada (2:18-cv-01476, filed 8 August 2018), alleging trademark/trade-dress infringement and copying of SeekingArrangement's marketing language; the docket ran until at least 2022 [15].

## Outcomes

- Short term, the shock campaigns delivered exactly what shock campaigns deliver: global name recognition at trivial media cost, and company-claimed rapid sign-up growth (60,000 members in six weeks; three-quarters female — company figures, unverified) [10][19].
- Medium term, the same campaigns destroyed the brand's largest asset — market access. The site was blocked at ISP level in Belgium and permanently confiscated there [4][11]; French ministers and the Paris prosecutor treated it as a pimping matter [5][6]; the founder acquired a criminal conviction personally [4]. The withdrawal of campaigns under pressure in Belgium and Norway also signalled that the company could not sustain its own tactics.
- Long term, the business did not survive. The domain now serves a bare shutdown notice ("RichMeetBeautiful has been shut down"); the Wayback Machine shows that notice live by December 2022, and Trustpilot reviewers reported an abrupt shutdown with unrefunded subscriptions around 2023-2024 [16][17][24]. Affiliate review sites claim the service "rebranded" as EliteMeetsBeauty; this is unverified, the sourcing is low quality and internally contradictory (one such site names Ashley Madison as the parent), so it should be treated as reportedly at best [23]. No reporting was found of any official statement explaining the closure.
- The category survived without them: SeekingArrangement and others continued operating in the UK and elsewhere, suggesting RichMeetBeautiful's failure was execution- and legal-risk-driven rather than a category failure.

## Lessons

- Earned media from outrage is real but it is a loan, not a grant. RichMeetBeautiful got global coverage for the cost of a few trailer billboards, then repaid it with fines (~265,000 euros plus costs), a criminal record for the founder, an ISP-level ban in an entire market, and eventual shutdown [4][16].
- The line that got crossed was not "sugar dating advertising" in general — it was three specific aggravations stacked together: (1) explicitly targeting a financially vulnerable, defined group (female students aged 18-26), (2) making the money-for-intimacy exchange explicit in the copy ("no student loans" / "improve your lifestyle"), and (3) physically ambushing that group at their place of study. Each element was cited by regulators and courts; the Belgian conviction was for the incitement framing, not for operating a dating site [1][4][5].
- In prostitution-law jurisdictions (Belgium's debauchery provisions, France's proxenetisme, Norway's hallikparagraf), ad copy that monetises intimacy can convert an advertising problem into a criminal one, with personal liability for directors. Vedal was prosecuted personally, not just corporately [1][4].
- Regulators punish the ad, but courts can punish the platform: Belgium escalated from ad-watchdog condemnation (JEP) to ISP blocking to permanent confiscation of the site in-country. A single campaign can cost an entire national market [4][11][12].
- "Gender-neutral" and "we prohibit paid sex in our T&Cs" defences failed everywhere they were tried. The Brussels prosecutor's point — the overall impression of the creative and the site, not the disclaimer, is what gets judged — is directly transferable to UK ASA practice, which also judges overall impression [1][10].
- Withdrawing a campaign under pressure does not stop criminal exposure. Vedal pulled the Belgian ads within days; he was convicted eighteen months later for having run them [4][20][21].
- For a UK entrant specifically: there is no RichMeetBeautiful ASA precedent, but the ASA's 2019 harmful-gender-stereotypes rules and its 2024 Seeking.com ban mark the current UK line — ads implying young women's value lies in youth/appearance traded against an older man's wealth are now banned territory even without any prostitution framing. Campus-adjacent OOH targeting students with debt-relief messaging would likely also engage CAP rules on responsibility, financial pressure and targeting, and — in the worst case framing — s.52-53 Sexual Offences Act 2003 (causing/inciting prostitution for gain). Do not touch student debt as a purchase trigger for intimacy-adjacent products.
- Repeating a banned stunt in a new jurisdiction multiplies rather than resets risk: each prior ban became evidence of intent and bad faith in the next country's coverage and proceedings [1][5].
- Cheap formats invite escalation because they are seizable: trailer billboards were confiscated in Oslo, Brussels and Paris within days each time — the medium gave authorities a fast, visible enforcement win, which itself fuelled the negative cycle [5][6][9].
- Watch the affiliate ecosystem: years after shutdown, affiliate "review" sites still publish fake-fresh reviews of the dead brand. For a legitimate operator, affiliate channels in this vertical carry compliance and brand-integrity risk that needs active policing [16][23].
