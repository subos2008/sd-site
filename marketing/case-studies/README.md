# Competitor case studies

How known competitors in (and adjacent to) this category launched and
acquired users, given the same constraint set we face: no Meta/Google ads,
no app stores, high-risk payments. Each study follows the same structure —
overview, launch, marketing playbook, platform/regulatory run-ins, outcomes,
lessons — and cites sources; claims that only trace back to the company
itself are marked as such.

Read these against [../channels.md](../channels.md) and
[../launch-plan.md](../launch-plan.md): the channel rankings there are
substantially justified by what worked and failed below.

## The studies

## Cross-cutting conclusions

What holds across all six studies, and what it changes in our plans:

1. **Nobody in this category ever grew on Meta/Google ads — the constraint we
   planned around is the historical norm, not a new obstacle.** Every durable
   player was built on some mix of SEO, earned media, affiliates, and
   product-led marketplace mechanics. Our channel ranking in
   [../channels.md](../channels.md) matches the empirical record.
2. **Web-first survived; store-first died.** The single sharpest pattern.
   SugarDaddie.com (2002), Seeking (2006), SugarDaddyMeet (2007) and Secret
   Benefits (no app at all) are still trading; the app cohort was erased by a
   policy paragraph with 30 days' notice, and Google's ban keyed off an app's
   stated purpose, not user behaviour. Validates the PWA architecture, and
   also warns that positioning language ("compensated", "arrangement") is
   itself a platform-risk surface.
3. **There are two proven acquisition playbooks, and they are complements.**
   Loud (Seeking, Ashley Madison, Reflex): founder-fronted data PR,
   engineered controversy, annual press franchises. Quiet (Secret Benefits,
   SugarDaddyMeet): affiliates and review-site placement fighting the
   non-brand SEO war while the operator harvests branded search. The quiet
   playbook alone built a top-3 player; the loud one built the category
   leader. Prudent plan: run the quiet engine as the base, add a modest,
   defensible earned-media layer (data PR, named spokesperson) on top.
4. **Data PR is the category's highest-ROI channel — with honest numbers.**
   Seeking's university rankings, Ashley Madison's city league tables, and
   WhatsYourPrice's average-bid tables each generated years of national plus
   hyper-local coverage from one templated annual release. Every one of them
   also eventually drew scrutiny for unverifiable figures. Publish only
   numbers we can defend; consider independent verification as a marketing
   asset (Ashley Madison had to buy E&Y reports after the fembot scandal —
   cheaper to be verifiable from day one).
5. **The student-marketing line is now precisely mapped.** RichMeetBeautiful
   stacked three aggravations — targeting a defined financially vulnerable
   group, explicit money-for-intimacy copy ("no student loans"), and physical
   ambush at their place of study — and got a criminal conviction, a national
   ISP block, and personal director liability, not just an ad ban. Seeking
   ran student offers for a decade without prosecution by keeping the
   proposition "dating" and the incentive structural (free premium via
   university email) rather than debt-relief copy. This is exactly the
   guardrail split in [../README.md](../README.md) ("The student
   demographic"): agency framing and structural incentives are survivable;
   desperation copy is what courts and the ASA punish.
6. **Never fake supply.** Ashley Madison's fembots were the category's one
   outright fraud finding — an FTC settlement and the permanent second line
   of every article about the brand. Cold-start density comes from seeding,
   pricing, and concierge onboarding ([../launch-plan.md](../launch-plan.md)),
   never from fabricated profiles.
7. **Affiliates are load-bearing and dangerous.** Every big player uses
   them; dead brands still get fake-fresh "reviews" years later, and rogue
   affiliate copy is an ASA liability that attaches to the advertiser.
   Approved-creatives clauses plus active policing are non-negotiable.
8. **Structural product decisions are marketing decisions.** Credits vs
   subscriptions (chargeback and regulator surface), free-for-women
   (supply-side liquidity), visible verification (trust as conversion,
   Online Safety Act groundwork), separating the provocative number from the
   revenue mechanic (WhatsYourPrice outliving its siblings). These belong in
   product plans, not just this directory.
9. **Treat all competitor member counts as fiction.** Self-reported
   cumulative sign-ups routinely run one to two orders of magnitude above
   independently observable signals. For our own comms: publish only metrics
   we would show a journalist's fact-checker.
