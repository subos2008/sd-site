# Case study: Ashley Madison

## Overview

Ashley Madison is a Toronto-based dating site for married people seeking affairs, launched in 2001-02 and operated successively by Avid Life Media and (since 2016) Ruby Life Inc. [1][2] It is not a sugar-dating business, but it is the canonical example of building a very large dating brand in a category that mainstream advertising channels actively refuse: TV networks declined its Super Bowl spots, transit authorities and regulators pulled its posters, and sports teams rejected its sponsorship money. The company converted those refusals into its primary growth engine — a repeatable "rejection-as-PR" machine fronted by a deliberately provocative CEO — supplemented by billboards, radio, data-driven press releases and a large affiliate programme [3][7][13].

The story has a hard edge that makes it doubly useful as a case study. The 2015 "Impact Team" hack exposed roughly 36-37 million accounts and revealed that much of the product's advertised value — an abundance of real women — was manufactured with tens of thousands of "fembot" fake profiles, leading to a 2016 FTC/multi-state settlement [15][17][20]. Company-issued numbers throughout its history should be treated with scepticism: leaked internal emails suggest executives fed inflated financials to business media [14]. Yet the brand survived the worst-case reputational event imaginable, rebuilt under Ruby, and by 2019-2020 claimed more members than before the hack [1][28][31].

## Launch and early growth

- The site was founded in 2001 (the site itself launched in 2002) by Darren J. Morgenstern, a Canadian entrepreneur. The name combined "Ashley" and "Madison", then two of the most popular girls' baby names in North America — chosen so the brand would sound like a person, not a porn site [1][2].
- Positioning was explicit from the start: rather than pretending to be a general dating site that tolerated married users, it owned the niche with the tagline "Life is short. Have an affair." The candour itself was the differentiator — competitors' married users were framed as liars polluting singles' sites, while Ashley Madison was "honest about dishonesty" [1][2].
- Noel Biderman, a former sports agent and lawyer, became CEO of parent Avid Life Media in 2007 and drove the aggressive growth era [35]. Under Biderman the business model was pay-per-communication credits for men (women free), a paid "priority" placement, and later a paid "Full Delete" feature — the last of which became central to the hack and the FTC case [13][15][18].
- Growth compounded through the 2008-2014 period: the company claimed 20 million members across ~30 countries by mid-2013 [27] and 37 million by 2015 [15]. These are company claims (registered accounts, not active users) and were never independently audited in this period; treat them as marketing numbers.

## Marketing playbook

### Rejection-as-PR

The core, deliberate tactic: produce an ad or an offer that a gatekeeper is almost certain to refuse, then merchandise the refusal.

- Super Bowl: NBC rejected an Ashley Madison spot for Super Bowl XLIII (2009) [1][4]; Fox rejected a 2011 spot (featuring adult-film actress Savanna Samson) as "not acceptable to air", with Biderman publicly demanding an explanation and accusing the NFL of discriminating against adult performers [3][6]. Canada's CTV likewise pulled an Ashley Madison ad from its Super Bowl broadcast [6]. Adweek noted that submitting racy ads certain to be rejected, then pitching coverage of the "surprising" rejection, was standard operating procedure for the company [3]. When it could not get on air, it bought what it could adjacent to the event — e.g. a sleazy print ad in USA Today's Super Bowl edition — to keep the "banned from the Super Bowl" narrative alive [5].
- Biderman as lightning rod: he appeared constantly on news and daytime TV (Tyra Banks, The View, Larry King Live), often with his own wife, to debate the morality of the site — every ethics segment was free reach [35]. His own marketing team described him as "the most hated man on the internet", and he leaned into the persona (publicity shots with lipstick-stained collars) [32][35]. The insight: for a product everyone has an opinion about, a debatable spokesperson gets booked where an ad can never run.
- The refusals extended beyond TV: in 2009 the Toronto Transit Commission voted down a CA$200,000 ad buy — which itself became a news cycle [1].

### OOH

Where paid media would take the money, Ashley Madison bought it — selectively and for shock value rather than coverage.

- The company ran only a handful of US billboards at any time ("seven or eight", by its own account), concentrated in large markets: Los Angeles (styled as a movie poster), New York (a hotel-room door-hanger visual, and a board reading "Who's watching your husband tonight?"), and opportunistic placements like Atlantic City, targeted on the theory that gamblers are comfortable with risk [34]. Each board was designed to be photographed, complained about, and written up — OOH as a PR prop, not a reach medium.
- The same mechanism worked in reverse abroad: an Australian "Have an affair" billboard was pulled after Advertising Standards Board complaints, generating another round of coverage [33].

### Stunts

- Naming rights: a documented $25 million offer (letter to the CEO, released to press) for five-year naming rights to the New Meadowlands Stadium — future Super Bowl host, home of the Giants and Jets — in 2010 [7]; a $10 million offer to rename Phoenix Sky Harbor "Ashley Madison International Airport", declined by the city [8]. Neither had any realistic chance of acceptance; both were cheap, headline-rich, and cost nothing when refused.
- Sports sponsorship offers designed to be refused: a reported €1.5 million approach to Italian basketball club Virtus Roma and an $11 million jersey sponsorship offer to Australia's NRL Cronulla Sharks, both turned down and both widely covered [1].
- Scandal ambulance-chasing: after Eliot Spitzer's prostitution scandal the company ran a full-page New York Post ad about him, and thereafter systematised the tactic — publicly offering endorsement deals to figures caught in sex scandals, e.g. a $400,000-a-year "brand ambassador" offer to Herman Cain [9]. When Rush Limbaugh lost advertisers over the Sandra Fluke affair in 2012, Ashley Madison publicly offered to buy his vacated ad slots — Limbaugh's on-air refusal was itself the earned media [10].

### Data PR

- Annual "most adulterous cities" league tables built from sign-up data, localised for hundreds of outlets: Washington DC crowned "America's least faithful city" in 2013 with a quotable Biderman line about the political class [11]; the format still runs today (Columbus, Ohio topped the 2024 list, covered by Fox News and dozens of local stations) [12]. This is the cheapest channel the company ever operated — one press release generates national plus city-by-city local coverage, every year, without any ad approval process.
- Caveat: the underlying data is unaudited and self-serving, and post-hack analysis showed how unreliable the company's user statistics were [14][20]. Journalists ran the lists anyway.

### Affiliates

- Ashley Madison ran (and still runs) a large affiliate programme through adult-traffic networks such as CrakRevenue, offering CPA, CPL and revenue-share deals across 50+ countries — reported terms include roughly 70-75% of first sales, i.e. ~$28-56 per converted signup [30]. This gave the brand paid distribution across adult and dating traffic that mainstream ad platforms would never sell it.
- International expansion followed a launch-PR-plus-affiliate pattern: 37 countries by 2014; Japan (launched June 2013) reached 1 million members in 8.5 months, breaking Brazil's 10-month record — records the company itself announced as press hooks [25][26]. Refusals abroad worked like refusals at home: Singapore blocked the site before launch amid public outcry (another global news cycle), and the company milked the legal ambiguity of South Korea's adultery law [26].

## Platform and regulatory run-ins

### Ad refusals

The company was refused by NBC and Fox for Super Bowl spots (2009, 2011), by CTV in Canada, by the Toronto Transit Commission, by sports teams and stadium authorities, and had billboards and TV spots pulled by advertising standards bodies in multiple countries [1][3][4][6][33]. As documented above, these refusals were substantially self-inflicted on purpose and monetised as earned media. Note the dependency: this only works because journalists cover the refusal — the tactic consumes brand trust and press goodwill as fuel.

### The hack (July 2015)

- On 15 July 2015 the "Impact Team" announced it had stolen the entire user database and demanded Ashley Madison and sister site Established Men shut down within 30 days; in August it dumped 60+ GB of data — names, addresses, credit-card transactions, and internal emails — covering roughly 36-37 million accounts [15][16][17].
- A central grievance was the paid "Full Delete" feature: the company charged users (about $19) to erase their data, yet retained personally identifying information — a claim the FTC complaint substantiated [15][18].
- Consequences: Toronto police linked two unconfirmed suicides to the breach; Biderman's own affairs surfaced in the email dump and he resigned on 28 August 2015; the company settled ~two dozen class actions for $11.2 million in 2017 [15][32].

### FTC fake-profiles settlement (December 2016)

- The FTC and 13 states plus DC charged the operators with deceiving consumers and failing to protect 36 million users' data. The defendants paid $1.6 million (reported as $1.66 million split between the FTC and states; the larger judgment was partly suspended based on inability to pay), admitted no guilt, and were bound to a comprehensive data-security programme with third-party assessments [17][18][19][36].
- The deception counts are the marketing lesson: the FTC alleged Ashley Madison used "fembots" — fake female profiles, some built from dormant real accounts — to trick men into buying paid memberships [17][18]. Analysis of the leaked source code by Gizmodo's Annalee Newitz found 70,529 female bot accounts versus 43 male, bot-sent messages to about 20 million men, and roughly 11 million men engaged in automated chat, with engineer comments in the code scheduling "engagers" per US state [20][21]. In other words, the growth-marketing claim at the heart of the funnel ("thousands of women near you") was fabricated at industrial scale, and it converted directly into revenue — which is what made it actionable fraud rather than puffery.

## Outcomes

- Scale (company-claimed unless noted): 20 million members by 2013 [27]; 37 million at the time of the hack (FTC: 36 million affected) [15][17]; 60 million announced February 2019 [1]; ~70 million per a 2020 report [31]. Post-hack, the company commissioned Ernst & Young reports to rebuild credibility, which verified an average of 15,542 genuine new members per day and rising female activity — a direct answer to the fembot scandal [28][29].
- Money: Avid Life Media reported revenue of $78 million (2013) and $115 million (2014), with a 2014 ad budget of $34 million [13]. Sceptical note: leaked internal emails indicate the company fed exaggerated financials to Forbes and Bloomberg, so pre-hack figures deserve a discount [14].
- Brand recovery: Avid Life rebranded as Ruby Corp./Ruby Life in July 2016 under new CEO Rob Segal, dropped "Life is short. Have an affair." for "Find Your Moment", and launched the understated, dialogue-free "Moments" film campaign [22][23][24]. Within a year the company concluded that softening the brand blurred it — in May 2017 it reinstated "Life is short. Have an affair." and its married-dating focus [1][24]. The 2024 Netflix docuseries "Ashley Madison: Sex, Lies & Scandal" reportedly drove another sign-up surge, per company executives [31]. In February 2026 the brand repositioned again as a "discreet dating" platform ("Where Desire Meets Discretion") [1].
- Verdict: the taboo positioning proved more durable than the scandal. Demand for the category did not disappear because the brand was disgraced; being the only famous name in the niche was worth more than being liked.

## Lessons

Transferable to a legal UK dating startup in an ad-restricted category (no Meta/Google):

- Own the niche in plain language. Ashley Madison's honesty about what it was for made it the category's proper noun. A clear, slightly uncomfortable one-line positioning ("Life is short. Have an affair.") did the work paid media couldn't [1][2].
- Rejection is a channel — if you engineer it deliberately and cheaply. Produce the ad, make the offer, write the letter; the gatekeeper's "no" is the story. Super Bowl submissions, naming-rights letters and sponsorship offers cost almost nothing when refused [3][7][8]. UK equivalents: TfL ad refusals, ASA rulings, Premier League sponsorship rejections — each is a news hook, but budget real creative and legal review, and expect the tactic to decay as journalists wise up.
- Put a debatable human on air. A founder willing to defend the category in hostile interviews gets editorial reach no restricted advertiser can buy [35]. Decide in advance how much personal reputational risk the founder will carry — Biderman's persona amplified the fall when his own conduct surfaced [32].
- Data PR is the highest-ROI restricted-category channel. Annual localised league tables from your own (honest) data generate national plus regional coverage indefinitely, with no ad approval needed [11][12]. UK press loves a city ranking. But publish only defensible numbers — post-hack scrutiny of Ashley Madison's stats poisoned years of its PR [14].
- Buy the media that will take you: OOH in a few dense markets designed to be photographed and shared, radio, adult/dating affiliate networks with generous CPA — and treat each placement as a PR asset, not just reach [13][30][34].
- Make expansion milestones the story. "Fastest market to 1 million members" turned each country launch into coverage; even bans (Singapore) were converted into global press [25][26].
- The cautionary tale — never fake supply. The fembots were not a growth hack; they were consumer fraud that converted directly to revenue, produced an FTC/state settlement, and became the permanent second line of every article about the brand [17][18][20]. In the UK this conduct would sit squarely under the Consumer Protection from Unfair Trading Regulations/DMCC Act and ICO enforcement. If your marketplace has a cold-start imbalance, fix it with pricing, incentives and honest expectation-setting — and consider independent verification (the E&Y reports) as a marketing asset in its own right [28][29].
- Honour deletion promises literally. Charging for "Full Delete" while retaining data was the hack's moral core and a named FTC count [15][18]. For a UK startup, GDPR makes this existential, and in a sensitive category "we genuinely delete" is a marketable trust feature.
- Security is marketing when your users have secrets. The breach, not the taboo, nearly killed the company; two suicides were linked to it [15][16]. Budget security like a brand campaign.
- Scandal does not necessarily kill category-defining brands — but don't over-rotate the rebrand. Ruby's softened "Find Your Moment" era was reversed within a year because the blunt positioning was the moat [1][22][24].
