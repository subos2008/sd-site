# Case study: The app-era cohort and the app store purge

## Overview

Between roughly 2015 and 2021, a cohort of venture-scale "sugar dating" apps — Sudy (plus its spin-offs Sudy Cougar, Sudy Gay and Sudy Les), Spoil/"Spoil Me"-branded apps, SugarD, and app versions of web incumbents like SeekingArrangement and SugarDaddyMeet's "SDM" — built their entire distribution inside the Apple App Store and Google Play [5][19][1]. That distribution was revoked by the platforms themselves: Apple tightened and enforced App Store guideline 1.1.4 against apps that "facilitate prostitution" or operate as compensated-hookup fronts [7][8], and Google Play announced on 28 July 2021 a policy explicitly banning "compensated sexual relationships (i.e. sugar dating)," effective 1 September 2021 [1][2][4].

A note on dates: this purge is sometimes misdated to 2022 (including in some secondary write-ups). The contemporaneous reporting is unambiguous that Google's announcement was July 2021 with a 1 September 2021 effective date [1][2][3][4]. What did happen in early 2022 was the most visible market response: SeekingArrangement's rebrand to Seeking.com in February 2022, explicitly "cutting the sugar" [15][16].

The contrast that matters for a web-first entrant: the web-native incumbents — SeekingArrangement (2006), SugarDaddyMeet (2007, part of the SuccessfulMatch network), SugarDaddie.com (2002) — predated the app cohort, never depended on app store distribution for their core business, and all still exist today. The app-native cohort either vanished, retreated to sideloaded APKs and web apps, or rebranded into euphemism [13][15][17][18][19].

## Launch and early growth

**The app cohort.** Sudy is the best-documented example. Operated by Sudy Limited, a Hong Kong company, it began a beta in September 2015 and launched as a Tinder-style swipe app pairing wealthy men with young women, explicitly pitched at college students: CEO Kurt Hou told TechNode "we are shocked by the rising number of students who are dropping out of college due to affordability issues," and 54% of registrants during the seven-month beta were students. Independent reporting at launch put it at just over 10,000 members across the US, Canada, UK and Australia, with a sugar-baby-to-daddy ratio of about 8:1 [5]. Sudy later claimed "more than 4 million users" and self-described as "ranking top 3 in the Sugar Daddy Dating category of App Store and Google Play since 2015" — these figures are self-reported marketing claims repeated by affiliate review sites, and no independent audit of them exists; treat the 4M number as unverified [19][23]. Sudy Limited ran a portfolio play, shipping Sudy Cougar, Sudy Gay and Sudy Les as separate apps to own adjacent store-search niches [19]. Around it sat a long tail of similar apps — Spoil-branded "secret arrangement" apps, SugarD ("featured on TV, in magazines" per its own marketing — again self-reported), "Millionaire Dating App," and SugarDaddyMeet's own app, "SDM: Dating App for Seeking Pure Local Arrangement" [1].

**The web incumbents, contrasted.** SeekingArrangement was founded by Brandon Wade in March 2006 as a website; it reported 300,000 members by 2009, 2 million by 2013 (44% reportedly college students, per the company's own "Sugar Baby University" PR), 5 million by 2016, and claims 40M+ today — the later figures are company claims amplified by press, not audited numbers [15]. SugarDaddyMeet launched in 2007, owned and operated by SuccessfulMatch, a white-label dating network headquartered in Vaughan, Canada that ran hundreds of niche dating sites off one member pool; its claimed "7 million members" is likewise self-reported [13][14]. SugarDaddie.com was built even earlier, in 2002, by ex-bond-trader Steven Pasternack, and grew on daytime-TV publicity and web marketing a full decade before the app cohort existed [17]. The structural difference: the incumbents' registration, billing and messaging all lived on their own domains. Apps, where they had them, were a convenience layer.

## Marketing playbook

**The app cohort: ASO as the whole funnel.** The app-native players acquired users primarily through app store search — ranking for "sugar daddy," "arrangement" and adjacent queries in the Dating category, exactly what Sudy bragged about ("top 3 in the Sugar Daddy Dating category… since 2015") [19]. The portfolio strategy (Sudy / Sudy Cougar / Sudy Gay / Sudy Les) is classic ASO: multiple listings, multiple keyword surfaces, one backend [19]. Monetisation ran through the stores too: Sudy launched with freemium subscriptions (reportedly $9.90/month for sugar babies, $50+/month for sugar daddies) plus planned virtual gifts — in-app purchase mechanics that gave Apple and Google their cut and made the apps wholly dependent on store billing as well as store distribution [5]. Paid user acquisition was largely closed to the category: Google had already updated its advertising policy in February 2021 to prohibit compensated-dating ads, months before the Play Store distribution ban — so by mid-2021 the category could neither advertise on Google nor, soon, be listed by it [2].

**The web incumbents: SEO, PR and affiliates.** SuccessfulMatch's own site pitches SugarDaddyMeet not just as a consumer product but as a "billing management and affiliate platform" — the network grew through affiliate commissions paid to a sprawl of "best sugar daddy sites" review and comparison sites, a pattern still visible today in the dozens of near-identical ranking pages that dominate organic search for the category [14]. SeekingArrangement layered aggressive earned-media PR on top (the annual "Sugar Baby University" statistics releases that reliably generated student-debt coverage) [15], and SugarDaddie.com used talk-show publicity in the 2000s [17]. None of this depended on any platform's continued goodwill: the asset was domain authority, a member database, and an owned billing relationship.

## Platform and regulatory run-ins

**Apple.** App Store guideline 1.1.4 has long prohibited overtly sexual content and apps that "facilitate prostitution, or human trafficking and exploitation" [7]. After FOSTA-SESTA became US law in April 2018, enforcement pressure on the category rose; Apple reportedly began quietly removing sugar dating apps from around 2018, citing 1.1.4, though this happened without announcement and is documented mainly by the affected industry's own sites — treat the timeline as "reportedly" [8]. What is well documented: SeekingArrangement was pulled from China's App Store in May 2018 after state-media scrutiny [6], and in June 2021 Apple updated 1.1.4 to explicitly state that "'hookup' apps that may include pornography or be used to facilitate prostitution will be rejected," clarifying (per Apple) an existing rule rather than creating a new one — apps facilitating compensated sex "have never been allowed" [8][9].

**Google Play.** On 28 July 2021, Google announced an update to its Inappropriate Content policy prohibiting apps that facilitate "compensated sexual relationships (i.e. sugar dating)" — the policy text bans "compensated dating or sexual arrangements where one participant is expected or implied to provide money, gifts or financial support to another participant" [1][2][3]. Apps were given a roughly 30-day grace period; the ban took effect 1 September 2021 [1][4]. Google's statement attributed the change to "feedback we received from NGOs, governments, and other user advocacy groups concerned with user safety" and said it aligned Play with "other Google policies and industry norms" — i.e. with Apple and with Google's own February 2021 ads policy [2][22]. Vice identified the immediate casualties: SeekingArrangement's Android app (around 1 million downloads, ranked 34th in Dating), Sugar Daddy Meet, Millionaire Dating App, and SDM [1]. Coverage widely noted the asymmetry that Tinder and other mainstream apps where sugar arrangements also occur were untouched — the ban keyed off an app's stated purpose, not user behaviour [21].

**Regulatory/legal context for the incumbents.** The SuccessfulMatch network's biggest run-in was not about sugar dating at all: in October 2014 a Santa Clara County jury awarded $16.5 million ($1.5M compensatory, $15M punitive) against SuccessfulMatch over its PositiveSingles STI-dating site, for republishing members' profiles — including STI status — across hundreds of other network sites without meaningful consent, with the court finding its terms of service unconscionable [10][11][12]. Relevant here because it shows the network model's data practices, and because SuccessfulMatch absorbed a $16.5M verdict and kept operating SugarDaddyMeet for another decade — a web business's regulatory hit was survivable in a way the app cohort's platform hit was not.

## Outcomes

- **SeekingArrangement (web incumbent):** survived, but sanitised. Lost its apps (China 2018; reportedly the Western app stores thereafter), then in February 2022 rebranded entirely to Seeking.com as a "luxury dating" platform, with sugar dating and "mutually beneficial arrangements" now explicitly banned in its own community standards. Founder Brandon Wade stepped down as CEO in June 2022 (returning as co-CEO in 2024). The business continued on its own domain and billing [15][16].
- **SugarDaddyMeet / SuccessfulMatch (web incumbent):** survived. Its SDM app was a Play ban casualty [1], but the website continued and still operates, claiming (self-reported) 7M+ members across 20 countries, still fed by its affiliate network [13][14].
- **SugarDaddie.com (web incumbent):** survived; 24 years old and still online, never meaningfully app-dependent [17].
- **Sudy (app-native):** lost its store distribution and with it its acquisition engine. It now operates primarily as a web app at sudy.app, still using sugar branding there, while an app titled "Sudy - Elite Dating App" (a different package, com.sudy.dating.app, scrubbed of sugar language) exists on Google Play — the rebrand-to-"elite" manoeuvre. Whether the elite-branded listing retains any of the old user base or growth is unknown; there is no independent evidence it recovered its claimed pre-ban scale [18][19][23].
- **The long tail (Spoil, SugarD, SDM, Millionaire Dating App, etc.):** effectively vanished as store products. What remains on Play are euphemism apps such as "Sugar - Spoil Me Luxury Dating," whose own listing now protests that it "does NOT cater to any sugar relationships" and that "any form of compensated dating or companionship is forbidden" — sugar keywords in the title, disavowal in the description, which is an ASO ghost of the category rather than a business [20]. Other survivors retreated to sideloaded APKs distributed from their own websites, reaching only users motivated enough to bypass the store [2].

Net: every business whose primary asset was a store listing was either killed or forced into a rebrand of unproven effectiveness. Every business whose primary asset was a domain, an SEO/affiliate moat and an owned billing relationship is still trading.

## Lessons

- **Distribution you don't control can be revoked with 30 days' notice.** Google's policy went from announcement to enforcement in one month, with no grandfathering and no appeal path that mattered [1][2]. Any category that a platform's trust-and-safety, government or NGO stakeholders dislike carries this risk permanently — and dating adjacent to money is exactly such a category.
- **Store billing dependence compounds store distribution dependence.** The app cohort's IAP/subscription monetisation meant losing the store meant losing revenue collection, not just installs [5]. A web/PWA product with its own payment provider loses neither.
- **Web/SEO/affiliate moats aged well; ASO moats aged catastrophically.** SugarDaddie.com (2002), SeekingArrangement (2006) and SugarDaddyMeet (2007) all outlived apps that claimed millions of users, because organic search, affiliates and direct traffic have no single owner who can switch them off [13][15][17]. For a UK web/PWA-first entrant, this is validation of the architecture — though note SEO for sensitive categories has its own gatekeeper risk (Google organic/ads policy), just a slower-moving one [2].
- **Rebranding after a platform ban is defensive, not generative.** "Sudy - Elite Dating App" and Seeking's "cut the sugar" repositioning both traded away the category keywords that built them; Seeking survived because it had a strong owned brand and domain to retreat to, Sudy's recovery is unevidenced [16][18][19]. Better to build the compliant positioning from day one than to be forced into it.
- **Be sceptical of category user numbers.** Sudy's "4 million users," SugarDaddyMeet's "7 million members" and Seeking's "40 million" are all self-reported and circulated mainly by affiliate review sites with commercial incentives [13][15][23]. Independent signals (Vice's ~1M-download figure for SeekingArrangement's Android app; TechNode's 10K-member beta report for Sudy) are one to two orders of magnitude more modest [1][5].
- **Positioning determines platform treatment.** Google banned apps by stated purpose, not user behaviour — Tinder was untouched [21]. A legal UK dating product that avoids any implication of compensated arrangements in its name, store metadata, and marketing copy dramatically reduces its exposure; one that flirts with the keywords for acquisition inherits the category's platform risk even if the product itself is clean.
- **Legal exposure and platform exposure are different failure modes.** SuccessfulMatch survived a $16.5M privacy verdict [10][12]; the app cohort did not survive a policy paragraph. For a startup, platform policy risk is the sharper cliff — but the 2014 case is also a warning about network/data-sharing practices under UK GDPR if any white-label or multi-brand pooling is ever contemplated.
